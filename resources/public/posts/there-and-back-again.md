# There and back again
We have a lot of concepts to say that all things in life are cyclical. We already listened to something in chemistry class, physics and, of course, biology. However, in this first post, I believe this idea is very appropriate. 

I am a biologist, at least, that is what my degree says. During college, I really liked all the existing problems in biology, a lot of information waiting to be analyzed, mainly in genetics and ecology, but an infinite number of tables with a quality that would scare any accountant. In the middle of this darkness, a hero appears, I had my first contact with the R language and the beauty of using code to process all that mess. This event was the spark for my master's degree in bioinformatics. In my master's, I thought I would greatly develop my skills as a developer in the service of biology. The reality was that I developed something that solved my problem at the time, but today I wouldn't risk calling my past self a developer. I realized that I needed help improving my software developer skills. So my journey as a professional developer began and my career as a scientist was put on hold.

Years have passed, today (2022) at least five years, and I dare say that I learned to be a reasonable programmer. So, it's time to look to that young biologist who thought would be useful to science and try again. My idea here is to create a series of posts throughout the development of my P.h.D, starting with this [repo](https://github.com/chagasVinicius/ohara) whose intent is to replicate the [Aracne](https://github.com/califano-lab/ARACNe-AP) algorithm using [Clojure](https://clojure.org/) and [Clerk](https://github.com/nextjournal/clerk).
